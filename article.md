### AI in Power & Utilities: Many Modes and Many Models (part 3)
The first wave of artificial intelligence was narrow. It processed one type of input at a time. Early models were trained to handle either text or images or sound or numerical sequences --- but not together. Each model was specialized and isolated. That limitation has now shifted. We have entered the era of multimodal AI.


Multimodal models process different forms of data in a unified system. Text, images, time series, audio, video, and sensor logs can all be fed into the same architecture. The model does not treat these inputs as separate streams. It learns to understand relationships between them. It sees that a field report, a vibration signal, and a thermal image can all describe the same event. The result is a model that operates closer to how humans observe and reason. It connects modalities, not just patterns.

This shift opens new ground for utilities. Power systems generate data across every modality. Line inspections produce photographs and videos. Substation sensors produce time-stamped numerical logs. Fault records contain structured data and natural language notes. Drone flights generate aerial imagery. Customer calls and emails produce unstructured text. These are not theoretical sources. They are part of daily operations.

Until now, most of this data has remained siloed. Teams specialize by input type. Analysts work in their own tools, trained on their own data formats. Engineers review images. Operations managers read logs. Contact centers handle transcripts. Each dataset lives in a different system. Each insight stays local. The full picture rarely comes together in one place.

Multimodal AI can change that. It can link an audio signal to a time series anomaly. It can match an image to a maintenance record. It can combine a sensor reading with a textual dispatch note. The model sees across domains. It treats different modalities as pieces of the same story.

To unlock this potential, the data must be ready. That does not mean perfect. It means organized. It means available in formats that models can consume. It means aligned in time, labeled with source, and cleaned of duplication. These are not advanced steps. They are foundational ones. They mirror the habits utilities already practice with physical systems. You do not install a transformer without specifications. You do not commission a new line without diagrams. Data deserves the same discipline.

Multimodal readiness also means knowing what the model needs. Some models require paired data --- text with images or audio with transcripts. Others rely on embeddings that reduce high-dimensional data into vectors. This means metadata matters. Timestamps, locations, and asset identifiers are not optional. They are critical signals. They allow the model to group, compare, and align inputs from different systems. Without that structure, the model will guess. With it, the model will reason.

A common misunderstanding is that multimodal AI is only useful for consumer experiences. News coverage often highlights use cases in entertainment and design. Models generate film trailers, music mashups, or surreal artwork. These applications are impressive. They do not reflect the opportunity in the utility sector.

Utilities are already sitting on terabytes of visual data. Drone flights capture thousands of images of transmission lines. Fixed cameras monitor substations and equipment. Handheld devices photograph meters and poles. Until now, this data has remained underused. Storage has grown faster than processing. Multimodal AI provides a path forward.

You can now ask models to review all images from a service region and flag leaning poles. You can ask for corrosion. You can ask for broken insulators. You can create a map of priority repairs based on visual evidence. These are not futuristic tasks. They are available now with off-the-shelf tools and structured inputs.

The same applies to acoustic signals. Vibration patterns can indicate mechanical issues. Unusual noise signatures can point to failures. These signals can be correlated with historical outages, repair tickets, and maintenance notes. A multimodal system can be trained to associate certain audio profiles with specific asset failures. It can be used to detect those patterns in real time.

Customer service also benefits. AI can link the sentiment in a customer's email to their outage history and meter readings. It can generate a contextual summary for agents. It can recommend responses that are not just polite, but informed. The model sees the full customer footprint, not just the words on the page.

This capability scales beyond operations. Training, compliance, and reporting can all be enhanced by multimodal inputs. Imagine generating a safety report that includes images, logs, and a summary of prior incidents. Imagine building a simulation tool that visualizes the effects of a policy change across assets, personnel, and customers.

The value of these systems does not come from automation. It comes from acceleration. They allow people to work across formats without switching tools. They reduce time to insight. They surface connections that would otherwise stay buried. That is the strength of multimodal AI.

This also brings a warning. When data is messy, models become wasteful. They burn compute trying to make sense of noise. They return results that sound fluent but lack grounding. This is not a failure of the model. It is a failure of the input. Context must be curated, not assumed.

That is why the foundation matters. Utilities that build clean data pipelines will spend less on compute. They will see better accuracy. They will unlock use cases that others cannot reach. Readiness is not about installing AI. It is about building a structure that allows AI to do real work.

Multimodal models are here. They will continue to improve. The question is not whether the technology will be ready. The question is whether your data will be.

### **Not One Model, But Many**
The excitement around artificial intelligence often centers on one model. A single large system that can do everything. It can write. It can summarize. It can answer questions. It can generate images or solve equations. This model is presented as a general-purpose solution. The assumption is that the best AI is the biggest AI.

This view is misleading. It does not reflect how intelligence works in practice. It does not reflect how infrastructure functions. And it does not reflect how power and utility systems operate in the real world.

In every complex organization, there are many levels of decision-making. Line engineers work on site-specific tasks. System operators manage real-time flow. Planners look at long-term investments. Each role requires a different type of judgment. Each uses different tools. No single person is expected to understand or execute all tasks. The system works because responsibility is distributed and aligned.

Artificial intelligence should follow the same logic. We should not expect one model to answer every question, serve every department, and support every decision. We need different models for different jobs. Some models should specialize. Others should generalize. Some should predict. Others should retrieve. Each should serve a purpose.

In technical terms, this means designing systems around layered models. A retrieval model can surface historical work orders. A classifier can tag events by risk category. A forecaster can predict load or failure probability. A language model can summarize reports or respond to routine inquiries. Each model does one thing well. Each runs with the right level of compute. Each supports a workflow with clarity and precision.

When models are matched to tasks, efficiency improves. So does trust. Operators understand what the system is doing. They can validate the outputs. They can intervene when necessary. This is not artificial general intelligence. This is layered, applied intelligence. It reflects how real systems grow. Not through scale alone, but through design.

In many organizations, the opposite happens. A large language model is deployed everywhere. It becomes the answer engine for every question. This leads to brittle outcomes. The model starts to perform tasks it was never designed for. It returns responses that sound good but do not fit the business logic. It creates hallucinated values. It overconfidently recommends actions without understanding the context. These mistakes erode trust. They also waste resources.

Large models require more infrastructure. They draw more power. They increase latency. They need frequent updates to remain current. When overused, they become an expensive substitute for tools that would have worked better with less.

This is not a call to avoid large models. They have a place. They are useful for summarization, translation, explanation, and creative synthesis. When they are trained well and paired with the right inputs, they can extend human capability in powerful ways. The key is to treat them as one layer, not the entire system.

The goal is not to replace workflows. The goal is to enhance them. That means working backward from the task. What decision is being supported? What input is required? What output is needed? What level of precision is acceptable? What systems must this model integrate with? These questions shape the architecture. They determine which model fits. They determine where to apply compute and where to avoid it.

In a grid operations center, for example, you might use a time-series forecaster to predict short-term demand. You might use a lightweight classifier to prioritize maintenance tickets. You might use a language model to generate a summary of an outage event. Each function runs with the right model. Each produces an interpretable result. Together, they support the operator without replacing the operator.

This modular approach also creates resilience. If one model drifts or fails, the others can continue to operate. If a model becomes outdated, it can be replaced without disrupting the entire system. This is the same logic that underpins physical infrastructure. You do not redesign the entire grid to replace one transformer. You maintain independence between components. You version systems. You test before deploying.

Applied to AI, this logic creates maintainability. It creates observability. It creates clarity. It allows organizations to grow their intelligence incrementally, with control.

Another benefit is governance. When each model serves a clear purpose, it becomes easier to audit. You can trace its inputs. You can evaluate its outputs. You can monitor performance over time. You can explain to regulators, customers, and partners how the system works. This transparency is essential for trust. It is also essential for compliance.

In the power and utility sector, decisions carry weight. They affect safety, reliability, and cost. They must be made with confidence. They must be traceable. They must be aligned with physical realities. This is not a setting where one-size-fits-all AI will thrive. This is a setting where layered intelligence, built with purpose, will make the difference.

The best AI system is not the largest. It is the one that fits the work.
